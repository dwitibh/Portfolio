The Problem

📌 Core question from senior leadership: "We know we're in these accounts, but how much of their total budget do we actually own? And who has the rest?"

The business had deep relationships with its most strategic accounts but was operating without a clear picture of its competitive position within each one. Revenue was being reported by contract, but no tool showed what proportion of the client's total addressable spend those contracts represented.

The clearest illustration of the gap: one major airport client had a £300 million annual projects budget. The company was delivering £100 million of that work, a 33% share. The remaining £200 million was flowing to competitors across M&E, compliance, and other project categories the company was already positioned to serve. That gap was both the problem and the opportunity.

An existing whitespace report covered the top 250 accounts at a macro level, but it lacked the service line granularity and CapEx/OpEx segmentation needed to drive strategic account conversations. A new, purpose-built analytical layer was required, one that could answer not just what the company delivered, but how much more it could win within each relationship.

Why CapEx vs OpEx Matters

In facilities management, these two spend pools behave very differently. OpEx (Operational Expenditure) covers ongoing service contracts, cleaning, security, maintenance, typically multi-year agreements with lower churn. CapEx (Capital Expenditure) covers major projects: fit-outs, M&E installations, compliance upgrades, smart building retrofits. CapEx decisions are made in planning cycles, involve different procurement teams, and represent significantly larger individual contract values. Winning CapEx work requires a different commercial approach, earlier engagement, and different relationship maps. The platform needed to surface both pools separately.

Approach

1. Salesforce as the Primary Data Source

A critical early design decision was where data would come from. The primary data source was Salesforce CRM: rather than building a separate data-entry tool, the share of wallet layer was embedded directly inside the existing Salesforce account planning workflow. When account managers created or updated an account plan in Salesforce, they were already completing the data that powered the analysis.

This solved the adoption problem before it started. The data entry was not an additional task, it was part of the Salesforce workflow account managers were already doing. Each of the 15 core service lines was surfaced as a structured input within the Salesforce account plan, capturing delivery status, contract value, procurement type, and the client's appetite for change. Salesforce served as the single source of truth for all account-level data, feeding directly into the Power BI semantic model downstream.

2. The 15 Service Lines: and Why Taxonomy Matters

The platform mapped across all 15 core service lines in the business. One of the most important pieces of groundwork was establishing a clean, agreed taxonomy before any analysis was built on top of it. The key distinction: Integrated Facilities Management (IFM) is a delivery model, not a service line.

In commercial conversations, IFM is often listed alongside service lines, which causes misclassification. An account might use the IFM delivery model (one provider managing multiple services) but not have all services integrated, for example, a major pharma client might use IFM for facilities but procure security separately. Treating IFM as a service line would mask the real competitive picture.

3. RAG Scoring: Appetite for Change as the Priority Signal

For each service line entry, account managers rated the client's appetite for change , their openness to switching providers or adding new services, on a High / Medium / Low scale. This single field drove the RAG (Red / Amber / Green) colour coding across the platform, turning qualitative relationship intelligence into a prioritisation engine.

A high-value service line gap with a High appetite for change became an immediate commercial target. A line the company was already delivering with a Low appetite flagged a retention risk, the client was unlikely to switch, but any service failure would be amplified.

Dimensional Data Model

The Power BI semantic model was built around four core dimension tables, with FactServiceLineEntry as the central fact table that joined them all. This structure allowed the dashboard to slice any metric, wallet share, opportunity value, RAG distribution, across any combination of account, service line, sector, and time.

TableTypeKey FieldsPurpose
DimAccount Dimension AccountID, Name, Sector, TotalCapEx, TotalOpEx Master account register, top 40 strategic accounts
DimServiceLine Dimension ServiceLineID, Name, Category, IsCapEx 15 service lines with CapEx/OpEx classification
DimContract Dimension ContractID, StartDate, EndDate, ProcurementType Active and pipeline contracts with renewal visibility
DimSector Dimension SectorID, Name, Type (Business / Tech / Projects) Sector filter, enables cross-sector benchmarking
FactServiceLineEntry Fact AccountID, ServiceLineID, Status, AnnualValue, AppetiteScore, RAGStatus Central fact, one row per account per service line (600 rows)

Key DAX Measures

Three core DAX measures drove the most important calculations across the dashboard:

  • Share of Wallet %: [Total Delivered Value] / [Total Client Budget] per account, segmented by CapEx and OpEx pools separately
  • Opportunity Gap Value: sum of estimated available budget on service lines flagged as Opportunity or Gap with High/Medium appetite for change
  • RAG Priority Score: weighted composite of status, appetite, and contract end date proximity, used to rank accounts and service lines for commercial focus

Analysis & Findings

Service Line Penetration Across the Account Base

Mapping all 15 service lines across 40 accounts revealed significant unevenness in penetration. Core FM services (cleaning, security) showed high coverage. Projects-related lines, M&E, power, compliance sub-services, were delivered in fewer accounts, despite those accounts having substantial CapEx budgets in those categories.

The Sector Lens

Filtering by sector exposed a pattern that wasn't visible in aggregate: the company's projects penetration in the Tech sector was significantly lower than in Infrastructure and Government accounts, despite Tech accounts having the highest average CapEx budgets. This insight directly shaped where commercial teams prioritised resource in the next planning cycle.

The sector filter also revealed that Smart Workplace and Power & Grid, both newer service lines, showed the highest appetite for change scores in the Tech and Finance sectors, making them priority pursuit categories despite low current penetration.

⚡ Key finding: the £183M identified opportunity was not evenly distributed, 68% of it was concentrated in just 8 accounts, all of which had High appetite for change scores on at least one major service line.

What I Learned

Taxonomy is the foundation: get it right before you build

The most important work happened before a single DAX measure was written. Establishing a clean, agreed service line taxonomy, and enforcing the IFM-as-delivery-model distinction, changed how several key accounts were classified. If left uncorrected, the misclassification would have materially skewed the competitive analysis for at least six accounts and understated opportunity in CapEx categories by a significant margin.

Embedding data entry in Salesforce is an adoption strategy

Asking people to populate a separate system is a common failure mode for analytics platforms. By surfacing the data collection inside the Salesforce account planning workflow that account managers were already using every day, the platform bypassed the adoption problem entirely. The data improved as a by-product of normal Salesforce activity rather than requiring a separate effort to maintain.

Designing for extension, not replacement, builds trust

The existing whitespace report was a trusted, widely-used tool. The instinct might have been to replace it with the new, more powerful system. Instead, the share of wallet platform was built to sit alongside it, extending capability without disrupting what already worked. This preserved stakeholder trust and meant the new layer was embraced rather than resisted.

Appetite for change is a better signal than contract size

The most commercially useful insight the platform generated wasn't a revenue number , it was the combination of opportunity size with the client's appetite for change. A £20M gap in an account with low appetite is a long-term play. A £5M gap in an account actively looking to change providers is a this-quarter conversation. The RAG scoring logic operationalised that distinction at scale across all 40 accounts.
